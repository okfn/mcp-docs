# Two pilots

The same architecture was tested twice, in two countries, with two
institutions and two very different datasets. That was deliberate.

| | Brasil | Uruguay |
|---|---|---|
| **Partner** | Office of the Comptroller General (CGU) | AGESIC |
| **Dataset focus** | Parliamentary amendments, one of the most requested datasets on the national portal | National Energy Balance (BEN): energy imports, generation, installed capacity |
| **Question** | Can citizens ask in natural language and get answers traceable to official data? | Does the same architecture hold in a different country, context and data environment? |

## Why two

One pilot proves a prototype can be built. Two pilots start to show
whether the approach is portable: whether the same MCP-based
architecture survives different datasets, different institutions and
different user needs.

That portability is the whole point of calling this an
[open model](../overview/open-model.md) rather than a product. If it
only worked on the energy balance, it would be an energy tool.

## How we tested

- **13 testers** in total.
- Testers from **inside the pilot governments**, who know the data and
  the institutional context.
- Testers from **civil society organisations**, acting as external
  validators, without the insider knowledge that makes an answer look
  right.
- Testers asked their own questions and recorded feedback in logs.
- The logs were then analysed for accuracy, recurring issue patterns and
  usability signals.

## What we set out to learn

**Accurate and verifiable answers.** Does the system return correct
answers? Can users check them against the source? Is it clear where the
information came from?

**Behaviour over longer conversations.** Does quality hold across five
to ten follow-up questions, or do context loss, contradictions and
unsupported interpretations creep in? This is a genuinely open question:
most testing naturally happens one question at a time, and multi-turn
degradation is harder to spot and harder to measure. We do not have a
confident answer yet.

**Usability and adoption.** Is the interface intuitive for people who
are not technical and not domain experts?
